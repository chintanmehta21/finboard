===== SEARCH [1] STAGE 1A: Cumulative Cash Conversion Ratio (CCR) — Indian Equities =====

Sources:
- Corporate Finance Institute: https://corporatefinanceinstitute.com/resources/accounting/cash-conversion-ratio/
- Wall Street Prep: https://www.wallstreetprep.com/knowledge/cash-conversion-ratio/
- Tickertape (India forensic checks): https://www.tickertape.in/blog/forensic-checks-to-avoid-fraudulent-companies/
- Dr Vijay Malik (CFO analysis): https://www.drvijaymalik.com/understanding-cash-flow-from-operations-cfo/
- Screener.in (CCR screens): https://www.screener.in/screens/890054/cash-conversion-ratio/
- Financial Edge (CCR guide): https://www.fe.training/free-resources/accounting/cash-conversion-ratio/

Key Findings:
- CCR = Operating Cash Flow / EBITDA; measures how well a company converts accounting profits into actual cash
- GOOD CCR range: 0.8 to 1.2 (1.0 = perfect conversion)
- MINIMUM acceptable CCR: 0.70 (70%) — Tickertape (India) specifically recommends cumulative CFO/cumulative EBITDA >= 70%
- Cumulative approach: Sum CFO over 5-10 years / Sum EBITDA over same period to smooth year-to-year noise
- EBITDA is pre-interest/pre-tax; CFO is post-interest/post-tax, so high interest/tax burden naturally lowers the ratio
- A persistently low cumulative CCR (well below 0.70) is a forensic red flag for earnings manipulation
- Working capital changes are the main driver of CCR < 1.0 in growing companies
- Complementary forensic checks: NFA turns < 1 for prolonged period = capital intensity concern; also check cash taxes vs PBT

Recommendation for QuantSystem:
- USE cumulative 5-year CFO/EBITDA as the primary CCR forensic gate
- THRESHOLD: >= 0.70 (70%) for pass; flag stocks below 0.60 as high risk
- Marcellus uses 6-year cumulative CFO/EBITDA — consider aligning with this standard
- Account for sector differences: infrastructure/utilities will naturally have lower CCR due to capital intensity


===== SEARCH [2] STAGE 1A: J-Score (CA Mayur Joshi / Indiaforensic) =====

Sources:
- Indiaforensic (official): https://indiaforensic.com/certifications/forensic-rating-model/
- Indiaforensic (J-Score article): https://indiaforensic.com/financial-statement-frauds-india/
- RegTech Times: https://regtechtimes.com/forensic-rating-model-and-the-importance-of-j-scores
- CA Mayur Joshi profile: https://indiaforensic.com/mayur-joshi-founder-of-indiaforensic-center-of-studies/

Key Findings:
- J-Score is a mathematical model using multiple weighted financial ratios to detect financial irregularities
- Created by CA Mayur Joshi in 2011, inspired by the Satyam scam (2009)
- THRESHOLD: Average J-Score of Indian traded companies is ~0.59; J-Score > 1.0 = BAD (creative accounting suspected)
- DATA REQUIREMENT: Minimum 2 fiscal years of company performance data
- DETECTION RATE: 78% accuracy in identifying creative accountants among NSE firms
- CORE ASSUMPTION: Cash flows are difficult to manipulate; companies manipulate profits via:
  (a) Accelerating sales recognition
  (b) Increasing cost deferrals
  (c) Raising accruals
  (d) Reducing depreciation
- PRESUMPTION: Companies are incentivized to manipulate when they have high sales growth + deteriorating gross margins + rising operating expenses + rising leverage
- VALIDATION: Successfully flagged Cox & Kings and PC Jewelers as suspicious; cleared Infosys, TCS, Reliance
- The model works globally but has provided best results on Indian listed companies
- Based on 2008 Indiaforensic research that identified 1,200+ Indian companies potentially using creative accounting

Recommendation for QuantSystem:
- CONSIDER implementing J-Score as a supplementary forensic check alongside M-Score and CCR
- Use J-Score > 1.0 as a hard rejection gate (consistent with Indiaforensic's threshold)
- The J-Score's focus on cash flow manipulation detection complements the CCR check
- Exact formula coefficients are proprietary; consider using the M-Score (Beneish) as a public proxy with similar logic
- Minimum 2 years of data requirement aligns well with the system's existing data windows


===== SEARCH [3] STAGE 1A: Marcellus MeritorQ Forensic Accounting Methodology =====

Sources:
- Marcellus (forensic article): https://marcellus.in/newsletter/meritorq-pms/meritorq-forensic-accounting-using-quant-methods-boosts-returns/

- Marcellus (accounting quality): https://marcellus.in/newsletter/little-champs/the-importance-of-accounting-quality/

Key Findings:
- Marcellus uses 11 forensic accounting ratios covering income statement, cash flow, balance sheet, NPA recognition, and audit quality
- Inspired by Howard Schilit's "Financial Shenanigans" (4th edition, 2018)
- DATA WINDOW: At least 6 years of historical financials
- RANKING METHOD: BSE 500 companies ranked D1-D10 (deciles) on each of 11 ratios; ranks cumulated for final pecking order
- TOP DECILES (D1-D3): Cleanest companies; MIDDLE (D4-D6): Questionable; BOTTOM (D7-D10): Fraudulent risk
- KEY RATIO: 6Y Cumulative CFO / 6Y Cumulative EBITDA (cross-cycle cash conversion)
- VALUATION: Uses P/FCF (Price-to-Free-Cash-Flow), shown to be superior to P/E, EV/EBITDA, P/B in India
- PORTFOLIO: 35-45 stocks from BSE 500 (excluding insurance), reconstituted quarterly
- HOLDING PERIOD: Stocks typically held for at least 1 year
- CLEAR EVIDENCE: Deterioration in returns and rapid increase in risk/drawdowns as you move toward inferior accounting quality deciles
- Forensic screening adds significantly to overall risk-adjusted returns and helps avoid value traps

Recommendation for QuantSystem:
- ADOPT the Marcellus approach of using 6-year cumulative CFO/EBITDA as the primary cash conversion metric
- Consider implementing a decile-ranking approach across the NSE 500 universe for forensic quality scoring
- Use the bottom 4 deciles (D7-D10) as a hard exclusion zone
- The 11-ratio framework is aspirational; prioritize implementing the most impactful ratios first:
  (1) Cumulative CFO/EBITDA, (2) Cash flow vs. profit divergence, (3) Audit quality flags
- P/FCF as a valuation measure aligns well with the forensic quality focus

- VEDANTA: Promoter pledge reached 99.99% of shares
- SEBI DISCLOSURE REQUIREMENTS:
  - Promoters must disclose pledging within 2 working days
  - Must report percentage pledged at promoter and group level
  - Must provide reasons for pledging
  - Quarterly filings required with pledged share data

  - 0-10%: Generally safe
  - 10-30%: Moderate; monitor trends
  - 30-50%: Elevated risk; needs due diligence
  - >50%: RED FLAG — institutional investors flag this level
  - >80-100%: Extreme risk; serious governance concern
- NO ABSOLUTE LEGAL CAP from SEBI on pledge percentage
- Post-Satyam (2009), SEBI mandated disclosure of pledged shares for all listed companies
- SEBI 3-year sunset clause: If a company's market cap drops below the threshold for 3 consecutive years, certain compliance requirements are relaxed

Recommendation for QuantSystem:
- SET promoter pledge gate at < 5% of promoter holding (current system spec is correct and conservative)
- DELTA CHECK: Flag if pledge increases by > 2 percentage points quarter-over-quarter (current spec: delta < 2pp)
- HARD REJECT at > 20% pledge — well below the institutional 50% red flag but providing safety margin
- Data source: NSE corporate filings portal provides pledged share data quarterly
- Consider adding a "governance risk score" that combines pledge data with board independence, related party transactions

===== SEARCH [5] STAGE 1B: Amihud Illiquidity Ratio — Indian Equities NSE Validation =====

Sources:
- PMC/PLoS ONE (Indian stock market liquidity): https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC7473572/
- ScienceDirect (Momentum and liquidity India): https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S0927538X23002640
- Amihud (2002) original paper: https://www.cis.upenn.edu/~mkearns/finread/amihud.pdf
- ResearchGate (Amihud premium analysis): https://www.researchgate.net/publication/228315879_An_Analysis_of_the_Amihud_Illiquidity_Premium

Key Findings:
- FORMULA: ILLIQ = Average of daily |Return| / Dollar Trading Volume over the year
- LOWER ratio = higher liquidity = better market breadth
- Amihud is regarded as the best price impact measure (Goyenko et al.)
- INDIA SPECIFIC:
  - India is among the MOST ILLIQUID markets globally — average Amihud ratio is 3.252x that of the US
  - India has the LARGEST illiquidity premium and risk-adjusted illiquidity premium globally
  - Study validated on NIFTY 500 constituents using data from April 2009 to March 2019
- NIFTY 500 STUDY: Used Share Turnover, Amihud Ratio, Relative Quoted Spreads, and Coefficient of Elasticity
- MOMENTUM-LIQUIDITY INTERACTION:
  - When conditioned on liquidity, momentum returns are more significant for the MOST LIQUID portfolio
  - Short/intermediate-term reversals documented among the most illiquid portfolios
  - Sample: 3,956 BSE stocks, 2000-2021
- FIRM VALUE: Amihud illiquidity has a significant INVERSE effect on firm value (higher illiquidity = lower valuation)
- Turnover ratio exerts a direct positive impact on firm value; Amihud and bid-ask spread have inverse effects

Recommendation for QuantSystem:
- USE Amihud illiquidity ratio as a liquidity filter for the NSE 500 universe
- EXCLUDE stocks in the top decile of illiquidity (most illiquid) — these are more prone to momentum reversals
- Given India's high illiquidity premium, this filter is especially important for the momentum factor
- FORMULA for daily calculation: |daily_return| / (daily_volume_INR)
- Average over trailing 6 months for stability
- Consider using relative Amihud (stock vs. sector median) rather than absolute to account for sector differences


===== SEARCH [6] STAGE 1B: NSE Impact Cost Calculation Methodology =====

Sources:
- NSE India (official): https://www.nseindia.com/static/products-services/indices-impact-cost
- NSE Clearing (margin categorization): https://www.nseclearing.in/risk-management/capital-market/categorisation-of-stocks-for-imposition-of-margins
- Angel One (impact cost): https://www.angelone.in/knowledge-center/share-market/the-impact-of-impact-cost
- BSE India (mean impact cost): https://www.bseindia.com/markets/equity/eqreports/impact_cost.htm
- Bajaj Broking: https://www.bajajbroking.in/knowledge-center/what-is-impact-cost-in-trading

Key Findings:
- FORMULA: Ideal Price = (Best Buy + Best Sell) / 2; Impact Cost = (Actual Price - Ideal Price) / Ideal Price * 100
- MEASUREMENT: 4 random snapshots per day from order book, over past 6 months
- CAPITAL MARKET: Impact cost computed for order size of Rs. 1 LAKH
- F&O SEGMENT: Impact cost computed for order size of Rs. 5 LAKH
- CRITICAL THRESHOLD: 1% impact cost
  - If mean impact cost moves from <= 1% to > 1%, the price scan range is scaled by sqrt(3)
  - Scaling drops when impact cost returns to <= 1%
- PERIODIC REVIEW: Calculated on 15th of each month, rolling 6-month basis
- Impact cost is computed SEPARATELY for buy and sell sides
- Impact cost VARIES with transaction size — larger orders face higher impact costs
- Impact cost is a HIDDEN cost beyond brokerage, STT, GST
- NSE publishes security-wise impact cost data regularly

Recommendation for QuantSystem:
- USE impact cost < 0.50% as the primary liquidity filter (more conservative than SEBI's 1% threshold)
- For portfolio sizing: Ensure position sizes don't exceed sizes where impact cost > 0.5%
- SOURCE: NSE publishes impact cost data — can be scraped or accessed via NSE archives
- ALIGN with NSE's methodology: Use 6-month rolling average for stability
- Impact cost is a better practical measure than bid-ask spread for the ATR-based position sizing in the system
- For a 1% risk budget, the impact cost should ideally be < 0.25% (one-fourth of the total risk budget)

  - Financials: Use different metrics (NPA ratios, CAR instead of D/E)

===== SEARCH [10] STAGE 1C: Earnings Quality Signals — Predicting 3-6 Month Returns in India =====

Sources:
- Chan, Jegadeesh, Lakonishok (Journal of Business, 2006): https://www.jstor.org/stable/10.1086/500669
- NBER Working Paper (accrual anomaly): https://www.nber.org/system/files/working_papers/w8308/w8308.pdf
- PMC (earnings quality measures): https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC4540158/
- Harshita et al. (PEAD India): https://www.scirp.org/journal/paperinformation?paperid=88060
- SSRN (capital investment/accruals South Asia): https://papers.ssrn.com/sol3/papers.cfm?abstract_id=3048931
- Taylor & Francis (earnings predictability India): https://www.tandfonline.com/doi/full/10.1080/23322039.2021.1898112
- Taylor & Francis (firm quality India): https://www.tandfonline.com/doi/abs/10.1080/10293523.2021.1991130
- Quantpedia (accrual anomaly): https://quantpedia.com/strategies/accrual-anomaly

Key Findings:
- GLOBAL CONSENSUS: High accruals signal low earnings quality and predict POOR future stock returns (3-12 months)
- ACCRUAL ANOMALY: Negative association between accounting accruals and subsequent stock returns (Sloan, 1996)
- Earnings increases accompanied by HIGH accruals = poor future returns
- Modified Jones Model with operating cash flows has strongest association with returns (Cheng et al., 2012)
- INDIA-SPECIFIC FINDINGS:
  - MIXED RESULTS: Some India studies find expected negative accrual-return relationship; others (Sehgal) report POSITIVE
  - The accrual anomaly in India may behave differently from US/global patterns due to market-specific dynamics
  - Indian stock market efficiency is debated (weak to semi-strong)
  - Quality INCREASES (not absolute quality) drive returns in India
  - Profitability and safety are the most relevant quality attributes
  - Quality's explanatory effect on prices is partially subsumed by momentum in SHORT holding periods
  - Investors CAN anticipate earnings before quarterly disclosure — changes in revenue and core earnings are key signals
  - Earnings surprise improves stock price informativeness (BSE 500 study, 2006-2012)
  - Quality-only strategy FAILED to produce substantial risk-adjusted returns in India
  - Quality + Value combination generates positive, significant alphas and higher Sharpe ratio
- NO INDIA-SPECIFIC STUDY found focusing precisely on the 3-6 month prediction horizon using accrual/cash flow signals

Recommendation for QuantSystem:
- ACCRUALS as a standalone factor may be unreliable in India — use in combination with other quality measures
- EARNINGS REVISION BREADTH (current system spec): Price-reaction proxy approach is sound given the limited India-specific accrual evidence
- COMBINE quality with momentum: Quality alone underperforms but enhances momentum in India
- FACTOR WEIGHT: Quality factor should have lower standalone weight; consider integrating into the Forensic Quality factor
- For the 3-6 month holding horizon: Revenue change and core earnings change are the most predictive signals
- Consider implementing an "earnings surprise" measure using quarterly results vs. prior quarter

Sources:

- Stanford (Luxenberg & Boyd EWMM): https://web.stanford.edu/~boyd/papers/pdf/ewmm.pdf
- Columbia (volatility forecasting): https://www.columbia.edu/~amm26/lecture%20files/volatilityBehaviorForecasting.pdf

Key Findings:
- EWMA DECAY FACTOR (lambda):
  - Range: 0.94 to 0.99
  - LOWER lambda = more weight on recent observations = faster response
  - HIGHER lambda = more weight on historical = smoother estimates
- OPTIMAL DECAY BY HORIZON:
  - Daily risk models: lambda = 0.94 (RiskMetrics standard)
  - Monthly risk models: lambda = 0.97-0.98
  - 1-month forecast optimal: lambda = 0.98 (arXiv study)
  - RiskMetrics suggests 0.97 (ranked second with minimal MSE difference)
- HALF-LIFE:
  - Definition: Number of lags at which weight falls to half of current observation weight
  - VOLATILITY HALF-LIFE: 26 weeks (Bloomberg MAC models)
  - CORRELATION HALF-LIFE: 52 weeks (Bloomberg MAC models)
  - KEY: Use FASTER half-life for volatility than for correlations
  - Portfolio estimation: Half-life of 63 trading days (one quarter) used by Luxenberg & Boyd
  - Volatility targeting: Half-life of 20 days common for volatility scaling
- TIME-VARYING BETA:
  - Simplest: EWMA-based beta varies over time even with constant parameters
  - Advanced: GARCH models for conditional covariance/variance
  - EWMA beta captures regime changes in market sensitivity
- ADAPTIVE MODELS:
  - Short half-lives: Better in volatile markets
  - Long half-lives: Better in calm, slowly-changing markets
  - Consider using time-varying (adaptive) decay parameter
- THEORETICAL BACKING:
  - Foster & Nelson (1996, Econometrica): Proved conditions for optimality of exponential weighting for covariance estimation
  - Muth (1960): Proof of optimality for exponentially weighted average
- SEPARATE DECAY FOR VOLATILITY vs CORRELATION:
  - Empirically confirmed: Faster volatility half-life + slower correlation half-life = better estimates
  - Example: Vol HL = 26 weeks, Corr HL = 52 weeks

Recommendation for QuantSystem:
- IMPLEMENT exponentially weighted beta estimation for the portfolio beta cap (current spec: max 1.3)
- PARAMETERS:
  - BETA ESTIMATION: Use EWMA with half-life of 63 trading days (~3 months / 1 quarter)
  - VOLATILITY: Use EWMA with half-life of 20 trading days (~1 month) for ATR and vol targeting
  - CORRELATION: Use EWMA with half-life of 126 trading days (~6 months) for factor correlation checks
