TOPIC 1: MULTI-STAGE SCREENING PIPELINE — OPTIMAL FILTER SEQUENCE
========================================================================

KEY FINDING:
The consensus across practitioner and academic sources is a 3-layer funnel:
  1. Liquidity/Investability gates (cheapest to compute, highest kill rate)
  2. Fundamental quality gates (moderate kill rate, removes garbage)
  3. Technical/Momentum ranking (fine selection from quality universe)

This matches our current pipeline order (1A Forensic -> 1B Liquidity -> 1C Earnings
-> 2 Factor Ranking -> 3 Regime Overlay). However, the evidence suggests LIQUIDITY
should come BEFORE forensic gates, not after, because:
  - Liquidity filters are the cheapest to compute (just price/volume data)
  - They eliminate the most stocks immediately (often 40-60% of universe)
  - No point computing M-Score for a stock that trades INR 5 Cr/day
  - Schwab example: 2,800 -> 814 (71% kill) on first fundamental pass

EVIDENCE:
  - Schwab: "Price and market cap is a good place to start — it allows you
    to eliminate a lot of stocks right away" [schwab.com]
  - FactSet: 4-8 stages work best for funnel visualization; order matters;
    stage values should represent a logical progression [babson.edu/FactSet]
  - Capitalmind: "Adding 200-DMA to 'stay safe' makes the backtest prettier
    but each filter makes live performance worse. Simpler screeners
    consistently beat complex ones." [capitalmind.in]
  - PythonInvest: Fundamental screening narrows first, then technical
    screeners applied to manageable list of 5-6 candidates [pythoninvest.com]

OPTIMAL KILL RATES PER STAGE (for 500 -> 10 pipeline):
  Target: 500 -> ~250 -> ~80 -> ~40 -> 10 (ranked)
  - Stage 1 (Liquidity):    ~50% kill (250 remaining) — ADT, basic tradability
  - Stage 2 (Fundamental):  ~65% kill (80 remaining) — M-Score, D/E, earnings
  - Stage 3 (Ranking):      Select top 10-20 from ~80 via multi-factor scoring
  - Stage 4 (Regime):       Exposure adjustment, not further filtering

  Our current pipeline: [500] -> 51 -> 41 -> 39 -> 32 -> 10
  This is 90% kill in Stage 1A (forensic) which is aggressive. Consider
  whether liquidity pre-filter would reduce unnecessary M-Score computations.

PRACTICAL RECOMMENDATION FOR OUR SYSTEM:
  - Consider adding a "Stage 0" liquidity pre-filter BEFORE forensic gates
  - Move ADT check from Stage 1B to a new Stage 0
  - This reduces the number of M-Score computations by ~40-50%
  - Current order is defensible but computationally suboptimal
  - Keep total stages at 4-5 (research says 4-8 optimal)

TOPIC 2: HANDLING MISSING FUNDAMENTAL DATA
========================================================================

KEY FINDING:
Professional quant funds use a hierarchy of approaches, and the surprising
finding from recent academic research is that simple cross-sectional mean
imputation performs nearly as well as sophisticated methods.

EVIDENCE:
  - Bryzgalova, Lerner, Lettau & Pelger (2022, NBER Working Paper 30761):
    "Missing financial data affects over 70% of firms representing about
    half of total market cap. Firm fundamentals are NOT missing-at-random,
    invalidating traditional ad-hoc approaches." [nber.org]

  - Chen (2024, Journal of Financial Economics):
    "Simply imputing with cross-sectional means performs well compared to
    rigorous expectation-maximization methods" due to three facts:
    (1) missingness occurs in large blocks organized by time
    (2) cross-sectional correlations are small
    (3) missingness tends to occur in blocks by data source [sciencedirect.com]

  - QuantPedia: "The most common procedure is to exclude an observation
    altogether if any covariate is missing (complete cases analysis).
    Alternatively, researchers impute the unconditional mean for a missing
    characteristic." [quantpedia.com]

  - MICE Framework (Springer 2019): "Imputes fundamental data by utilizing
    the interdependency among variables complying with accounting rules,
    using Expectation Maximization methodology." [springer.com]

  - BlueChipAlgos: "Situations of missing data happen in cases of years
    of low liquidity for small cap stocks or equity instruments in
    emerging markets as plenty of the data is irregular." [bluechipalgos.com]

HIERARCHY OF APPROACHES (from simplest to most complex):
  1. Exclude stocks with missing data entirely (most common, loses signal)
  2. Cross-sectional median/mean imputation (surprisingly effective)
  3. Industry median imputation (better for sector-specific metrics)
  4. Last-known-value carry-forward (for time-series gaps)
  5. MICE / EM-based imputation (academic gold standard, complex)
  6. PCA-based matrix completion (cutting edge)

PRACTICAL RECOMMENDATION FOR OUR SYSTEM:
  - Our Stage 1C "lenient on missing data" approach is CORRECT and supported
    by research — excluding stocks with missing data biases toward large caps
  - For missing fundamental data in scoring: use INDUSTRY MEDIAN imputation
    rather than excluding or using zero
  - Apply a confidence penalty (see Topic 6) when data is imputed
  - For the NSE 500 universe, ~15-25% of mid/small caps will have patchy
    quarterly data; excluding them loses valuable signal
  - Current YoY fallback for cyclical sectors is a sound proxy strategy

TOPIC 3: BENEISH M-SCORE FOR INDIAN EQUITIES
========================================================================

KEY FINDING:
The -2.22 threshold is MORE CONSERVATIVE than the standard -1.78 and is
appropriate for our use case (gate filter, not primary signal). Indian
studies confirm the model works for NSE-listed companies but with caveats.

EVIDENCE:
  - Beneish (1999, original): -1.78 threshold detects 76% of manipulators
    with 17.5% false positive rate. -2.22 is the "40:1 cost ratio" cutoff
    that reduces false positives at the expense of missing more manipulators.
    [en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Beneish_M-score]

  - Shah et al. (Indian Journal of Finance): Study of BSE 100 companies
    (2011-2016) found 11 out of 65 companies showed manipulation signals.
    Three ratios most significant: TATA (accruals), DSRI (receivables),
    and SGI (sales growth). [indianjournaloffinance.co.in]

  - GMT Research (Asia study 2010-2015): "33% of companies across Asia
    recorded a Beneish M-Score greater than -2.22, although this dropped
    to just 16% if the threshold was raised to -1.78." This means at -2.22,
    one-third of Asian companies flag as potential manipulators — this is
    a HIGH false positive rate for a screening gate. [gmtresearch.com]

  - NSE500 study: "Indian companies with M-score above -1.78 report lower
    returns on average in the following year." Confirms predictive value
    for Indian equities. [abacademies.org]

  - Portfolio123: The model was not designed for financial companies —
    "sales and receivables, two main ingredients, are not used when
    analyzing financial institutions." [portfolio123.com]

  - StableBread: "-2.22 is more conservative and reduces the likelihood
    of false positives." [stablebread.com]

THRESHOLD ANALYSIS:
  -1.78 (standard):  76% detection, 17.5% false positive rate
  -2.22 (conservative): Lower detection (~50-60% est.), ~8-10% false positive

  For a GATE FILTER (not a scoring signal), we want LOW false positives
  (don't want to wrongly exclude good stocks). The -2.22 threshold is
  correct for this purpose.

  HOWEVER: GMT's Asia finding that 33% of companies flag at -2.22 is
  concerning. This suggests either:
  (a) Asian accounting standards trigger more flags (different GAAP)
  (b) The model needs recalibration for Indian/Asian equities
  (c) There genuinely is more earnings manipulation in the region

PRACTICAL RECOMMENDATION FOR OUR SYSTEM:
  - KEEP the -2.22 threshold — it's the right choice for a gate filter
  - EXCLUDE financial companies from M-Score calculation (as Beneish intended)
    — our SECTOR_DE_CAPS already handles banks differently
  - Consider a NaN-safe implementation (v0.2 bug was NaN bypassing gate)
  - Monitor the actual kill rate: if M-Score alone is killing >30% of
    the universe, consider loosening to -2.50 or -2.76
  - The 3 most predictive components for India: TATA, DSRI, SGI — could
    weight these higher in a simplified 3-variable model

TOPIC 4: 200 DMA FILTER & MOMENTUM IN INDIAN EQUITIES
========================================================================

KEY FINDING:
Momentum is the STRONGEST factor in Indian equities, with 20%+ CAGR over
long periods. The 200 DMA filter is widely used but evidence suggests it
may hurt live performance despite improving backtests.

EVIDENCE:
  - Nifty 200 Momentum 30 Index: 20.64% CAGR vs 14-15% for Nifty 50
    over 19 years, Sharpe 0.89 vs 0.57. Uses 6-month + 12-month price
    return adjusted for volatility. [niftyindices.com]

  - Capitalmind: "The Nifty 200 Momentum 30 compounded at nearly ~20%
    CAGR over five years." [capitalmind.in]

  - Research360: "52-week high screener with volume confirmation (1.5x
    average) and price above 200 DMA shows backtests with 18-22% CAGR."
    [research360.in]

  - CRITICAL WARNING from Capitalmind: "Adding 200-DMA to 'stay safe'
    makes the backtest prettier but each filter makes live performance
    worse. Simpler screeners consistently beat complex ones."
    [capitalmind.in]

  - Momentum in India study (ScienceDirect): "Relative momentum strategies
    yield large and substantial profits in the Indian Stock Market."
    [sciencedirect.com]

  - QED Capital: "Momentum emerged as the most effective strategy,
    delivering substantial returns and outperforming other factors."
    [qedcap.com]

  - NSE Methodology: Nifty200 Momentum 30 does NOT use 200 DMA as a
    filter. It uses normalized momentum score = (6M return + 12M return)
    adjusted for volatility. No price-vs-moving-average filter.
    [niftyindices.com]

200 DMA AS FIRST-STAGE FILTER:
  Pro: Easy to compute, removes downtrending stocks, improves backtest metrics
  Con: Adds regime-dependent whipsaw, removes mean-reversion opportunities,
       "makes backtests prettier but hurts live performance"

  The Nifty Momentum 30 index — the gold standard for Indian momentum —
  does NOT use 200 DMA. It uses pure return-based momentum with vol adjustment.

PRACTICAL RECOMMENDATION FOR OUR SYSTEM:
  - Do NOT add 200 DMA as a hard filter gate — evidence says it hurts live
    performance despite improving backtests
  - Our Mansfield RS factor already captures relative momentum vs benchmark
  - If you want a trend filter, use it as a SOFT SIGNAL in scoring (small
    weight) rather than a binary gate
  - The Nifty Momentum 30 methodology (6M+12M return / volatility) is
    essentially what our VAM factor already does
  - Momentum is confirmed as the strongest factor in India (20%+ CAGR)
  - Our MRS weight of 35% is well-supported by evidence

TOPIC 5: 52-WEEK HIGH PROXIMITY AS MOMENTUM FACTOR
========================================================================

KEY FINDING:
52-week high proximity is one of the MOST powerful momentum predictors,
often SUPERIOR to traditional momentum (past returns). The seminal George
& Hwang (2004) paper in the Journal of Finance established this conclusively.

EVIDENCE:
  - George & Hwang (2004, Journal of Finance): "Nearness to the 52-week
    high dominates past returns in terms of predictive power and largely
    explains momentum profits." The 52-week high measure has predictive
    power whether or not stocks have had extreme past returns. Returns
    forecast using 52-week high do NOT reverse in the long run (unlike
    traditional momentum). [wiley.com, bauer.uh.edu]

  - Performance: "Average monthly gain to the 52-week high momentum
    strategy (6-month hold) was 0.45% — about twice as large as those
    associated with other momentum strategies." More recent: 0.65%/month
    vs 0.38% for traditional momentum. [bauer.uh.edu]

  - Breakout statistics: "Stocks breaking through 52-week highs with
    volume exceeding 150% of 20-day average continue upward 72% of
    the time for an average gain of 11.4% over 31 trading days."
    [pocketoption.com]

  - Behavioral mechanism: Investors under-react to positive news about
    stocks near their 52-week high due to anchoring bias. When the high
    is finally breached, prices see further gains. [kavout.com]

  - International evidence: Works across international stock markets,
    not just US. [sciencedirect.com]

  - Screening thresholds commonly used:
    * Within 5-10% of 52-week high: aggressive momentum screen
    * Within 20-25%: moderate momentum screen
    * Our current system uses no explicit 52-week high filter

NEARNESS RATIO (Price / 52-Week High):
  - Ratio > 0.90 (within 10%): Strong momentum, breakout candidates
  - Ratio > 0.75 (within 25%): Moderate momentum, in uptrend
  - Ratio < 0.50 (>50% off high): Potential value trap or mean-reversion

PRACTICAL RECOMMENDATION FOR OUR SYSTEM:
  - Consider adding 52-week high proximity as a SUPPLEMENTARY factor or
    as a pre-filter in Stage 1B
  - It would complement (not replace) our Mansfield RS factor
  - Threshold: "within 25% of 52-week high" (ratio > 0.75) is a good
    moderate filter that our Research360 source confirms
  - For bearish screening: ratio < 0.50 could identify short candidates
  - This is CHEAP TO COMPUTE (just need 1 year of price data, which we
    already have for MRS calculation)
  - George & Hwang's finding that 52WH momentum doesn't reverse long-term
    is important — it means it's safer than traditional momentum for our
    3-6 month holding period

TOPIC 6: CONFIDENCE SCORING WITH PARTIAL/MISSING DATA
========================================================================

KEY FINDING:
The research supports a "available information weighting" approach where
composite scores are computed from observed components only, with an
explicit confidence penalty proportional to the fraction of missing data.

EVIDENCE:
  - BMC Medical Research (2025): "Instead of imputing a missing component
    value, that component can simply be discarded from the composite score
    formula and the composite score derived as the average of the observed
    components with modified weights that preserve the relations."
    [springer.com/BMC]

  - PMC Factor Score study: "Expected posterior-weighted full maximum
    likelihood methods are significantly more reliable than sum scores
    and regression estimators in the presence of missing data. The
    reliability can be closely approximated by missingness-adjusted
    communality." [pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov]

  - ArXiv (2025): "Score matching can be adapted to work with missing
    data in a flexible setting where data can be partially missing over
    any subset of the coordinates." [arxiv.org]

  - ScienceDirect (ensemble methods): "Use confidence scores of base
    learners — targeting training data for which existing base learners
    cannot output a confident prediction." [sciencedirect.com]

  - Practical quant approach: "When missing values exist in factors,
    imputation is performed using the industry median, and when the
    industry median is unavailable, the overall median of the stock
    pool is applied." [sciencedirect.com]

RECOMMENDED CONFIDENCE PENALTY FORMULA:
  confidence_multiplier = (n_available / n_total) ^ alpha

  Where:
  - n_available = number of factors with actual (non-imputed) data
  - n_total = total number of factors in the model
  - alpha = penalty exponent (0.5 = mild, 1.0 = linear, 2.0 = harsh)

  Example for our 4-factor model:
  - All 4 factors available: (4/4)^0.5 = 1.00 (no penalty)
  - 3 of 4 factors available: (3/4)^0.5 = 0.87 (13% penalty)
  - 2 of 4 factors available: (2/4)^0.5 = 0.71 (29% penalty)
  - 1 of 4 factors available: (1/4)^0.5 = 0.50 (50% penalty)

  With alpha=1.0 (linear):
  - 3/4: 0.75, 2/4: 0.50, 1/4: 0.25

PRACTICAL RECOMMENDATION FOR OUR SYSTEM:
  - Use alpha=0.5 (square root penalty) — mild but meaningful
  - Compute composite score from available factors only (re-weighted)
  - Multiply by confidence_multiplier
  - Set a MINIMUM threshold: require at least 2 of 4 factors to be
    available (50% minimum data coverage)
  - Track confidence scores in output — useful for portfolio sizing
    (lower confidence = smaller position)
  - This approach is better than our current binary pass/fail on
    missing data in Stage 1C

TOPIC 7: MINIMUM SAMPLE SIZE FOR FACTOR RANKING — PERCENTILE vs Z-SCORE
========================================================================

KEY FINDING:
For universes of 40-80 stocks, PERCENTILE RANKING is more robust than
z-scores. Z-scores assume normality and are sensitive to outliers, which
is problematic with small samples of financial data.

EVIDENCE:
  - Statistical consensus: "Z-score method will NEVER detect an outlier
    if the dataset has fewer than 12 items." For small samples, z-scores
    behave strangely. [machinelearningplus.com]

  - PMC (2025): "You should avoid using z-scores when displaying profile
    or group differences" — z-scores can be misleading when the
    underlying distribution is non-normal. [pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov]

  - Statology: "When normally distributed, any z-score can be associated
    with a specific percentage of the population. This does not work well
    if the data are not normally distributed." [statology.org]

  - Analytics Vidhya: "Converting values to their equivalent percentile
    rank is more robust against outliers and makes no assumptions about
    the underlying shape of the distribution, though it requires a large
    number of observations to be meaningful." [medium.com]

  - Pomona College thesis: "Converting each metric to a z-score or
    percentile rank normalizes data across industries." For stock ranking
    with 50 stocks, both approaches work but percentile is more robust.
    [pomona.edu]

  - S&P Global: "To create comparable data sets, fundamental data within
    a factor score is standardized into a z-score. Z-scores of different
    metrics can be averaged to provide a single quality score."
    [spglobal.com]

  - Medium (Malafosse): Z-scores are sensitive to extreme outliers —
    "if there are very large outliers in the dataset, this can skew the
    mean." Modified z-score (using median instead of mean) is more robust.
    [medium.com]

MINIMUM SAMPLE SIZES:
  - Z-scores: Minimum ~30 for Central Limit Theorem to apply; practically
    need 50+ for reliable standardization of financial data
  - Percentile ranks: Work with any sample size but become "coarse" below
    ~20 (each stock = 5 percentile points)
  - Modified z-scores (MAD-based): Work well down to ~15 observations
  - For N=40-80: Both methods are usable, but percentile is safer

COMPARISON FOR OUR PIPELINE (N = 32-80 after Stage 1):

  Z-Score Pros:
  - Preserves magnitude of differences (stock A is 2x better than B)
  - Better for combining factors via weighted average
  - S&P and MSCI use z-scores in their factor indices

  Z-Score Cons:
  - Assumes normality (financial factors are usually skewed)
  - Single outlier can compress the rest of the distribution
  - With N=32, a single extreme value distorts everything

  Percentile Pros:
  - Robust to outliers and non-normal distributions
  - No distributional assumptions
  - Uniform [0,1] output is easy to interpret and combine

  Percentile Cons:
  - Loses magnitude information (rank 1 vs rank 2 may differ by 0.1% or 10%)
  - "Coarse" with small N (each rank = 3.1 percentile points at N=32)
  - Can't distinguish between stocks that are very close in value

HYBRID APPROACH (BEST OF BOTH):
  1. Winsorize raw factor values at 2.5th/97.5th percentile (clip outliers)
  2. Apply modified z-score (using median and MAD instead of mean and SD)
  3. For the final composite, use percentile ranking of the composite score

PRACTICAL RECOMMENDATION FOR OUR SYSTEM:
  - For N=32-80 (our typical post-filter universe): use WINSORIZED Z-SCORES
    for individual factors, then PERCENTILE RANK for the final composite
  - Winsorize at +/- 3 sigma (we already do this for VAM — extend to all
    factors)
  - Modified z-score (median/MAD) is worth considering as a robustness
    improvement, especially during BEAR regime when universe is smallest
  - At N<20, switch to pure percentile ranking (forgo z-scores entirely)
  - Our current approach of percentile ranking within the surviving universe
    is sound; just ensure winsorization is applied to all factors first
