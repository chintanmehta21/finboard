Q6: Forensic Quality Score — Negative IC Problem
STATUS: EXCELLENT ANSWER
Source: Damodaran valuation courses + Buffett letters

FINDING: FQ should be a PASS/FAIL gate, NOT a linear ranking factor.

Evidence:
- Quality predicts LOWER returns because high quality = high valuations = limited
  upside. The market already prices in accounting cleanliness.
- Buffett: "A business with terrific economics can be a bad investment if the price
  paid is excessive."
- Damodaran: Expected returns come from "mismatches" — high ROE but low multiples.
  Pure quality screening finds expensive companies.
- The negative IC (-0.17 at 3M, -0.23 at 6M) is EXPECTED because forensic quality
  acts as insurance against blowups (prevents drawdowns) but does NOT catalyze
  short-term appreciation.

RECOMMENDATION:
- Remove FQ as a scoring factor in Stage 2
- Keep FQ as a binary gate in Stage 1A (pass/fail only)
- Do NOT replace with deep value (P/FCF) for 3-6M — Damodaran says short-term
  returns are "voting machine" driven, not "weighing machine"
- If adding a value signal, use RELATIVE valuation (sector-relative P/E or
  EV/EBIT) to find "mismatches" where quality is high but valuation is not excessive

Q12: QoQ vs YoY for Cyclical Sectors
STATUS: EXCELLENT ANSWER
Source: Damodaran valuation courses

FINDINGS:
1. Damodaran uses trailing 12-month (TTM) earnings — bundling last 4 quarters —
   as the most updated baseline. NOT quarter-over-quarter.
2. He explicitly warns that using the most recent year's earnings for cyclical/
   commodity companies is "highly misleading" because those numbers reflect
   extreme peaks and troughs.
3. To normalize: estimate what the business would generate in a "normal year" —
   this requires judgment, not a mechanical formula. Use common sense to smooth
   out booms and recessions.
4. KEY CONCEPT — "Truncation Risk": The danger that a distressed company won't
   survive long enough to see the cycle turn. Companies with heavy debt during
   a downturn face high probability of liquidation.
5. To distinguish temporary weakness from structural decline: LOOK AT THE
   BALANCE SHEET. Cyclical + high debt = potential death. Cyclical + low debt =
   likely to survive and recover.

RECOMMENDATION:
- Switch cyclical sectors from QoQ to TTM (trailing 12-month) comparison
- Add a "truncation risk" check: cyclical sector + D/E > sector median +
  declining revenue = HIGH RISK exclusion
- Use TTM revenue growth instead of QoQ for all sectors (more stable signal)
- For cyclicals, compare TTM revenue to same-period prior year (YoY TTM)

Q22: Regime Scalar — Sizing vs Ranking
STATUS: EXCELLENT ANSWER
Source: Buffett letters + Damodaran courses

FINDINGS:
1. Buffett: Rejects macro market timing as a "terrible mistake" BUT maintains
   massive cash reserves ($10-20B minimum, sometimes $100B+) as permanent defense.
2. KEY FINDING: Regime scalar should apply to SIZING, not RANKING. This preserves
   ranking quality (differentiation between candidates) while reducing total
   capital deployed in adverse regimes.
3. Damodaran: Uses a dynamic implied equity risk premium that naturally adjusts —
   in overvalued markets, fewer stocks pass valuation screens, so the portfolio
   naturally concentrates without needing to artificially compress scores.
4. Mathematically: if all scores are multiplied by 0.1 in BEAR, the relative
   ranking is preserved but the scores become compressed to 3-10 range, making
   differentiation difficult. Better to rank by raw confidence, then size by scalar.

RECOMMENDATION:
- Apply regime scalar (0.1 in BEAR) to position SIZE after ranking
- Keep raw confidence scores for candidate ranking/selection
- This mathematically preserves ranking quality while reducing exposure
- Alternative: use regime to set max number of positions (e.g., BEAR = max 2-3)

Q27: Defensive Sector Balance
STATUS: EXCELLENT ANSWER
Source: Buffett letters + Damodaran courses

FINDINGS:
1. Buffett defines defensive businesses as those with enduring "moat" — powerful
   global brand (Coca-Cola, Gillette) or low-cost producer status. Essential
   products/services that retain purchasing power during inflation while requiring
   minimal incremental capital.
2. He does NOT explicitly label FMCG, Pharma, or IT as defensive sectors. He
   actively AVOIDS industries prone to rapid change because their competitive
   moats are easily destroyed (this could include IT).
3. For surviving bear markets: Buffett does NOT rely on sector rotation. He
   relies on maintaining massive, untouchable CASH reserves (U.S. Treasuries)
   to ensure he never needs to sell assets during a panic.
4. HARD-ENFORCE CASH/LIQUIDITY in BEAR regime aligns closer to Buffett's
   philosophy than hard-enforcing sector caps.
5. Damodaran on emerging markets (India): Add Country Risk Premium (CRP) =
   sovereign default spread × (equity volatility / bond volatility). Apply based
   on actual revenue exposure, not just where company is incorporated.
6. Neither Buffett nor Damodaran designs risk controls for 3-6M horizons. Buffett
   considers short-term market timing irrational.

RECOMMENDATION:
- Don't hard-enforce sector caps for "defensive" allocation
- Instead, consider cash-weight rules: in BEAR, hold more in cash equivalents
- Our 10% exposure in BEAR already approximates this philosophy
- For Indian equities, incorporate country risk premium in valuation-based signals
- Defensive = businesses with pricing power + low capital needs, not specific sectors
